Work / Case study 02 / Location tracking for field teams

Designing trust into a two-sided location product — without surveillance creep.

Managers needed truth. Technicians needed dignity. I designed a geo-fence that nudges instead of polices, and replaced a dense table of rows with a live map of people.

Role
Lead designer · Dual-sided research
Team
1 designer · 3 eng · 1 PM
Surface
Mobile (tech) · Web (manager)
Year
2024 — Q4 ship

Enterprise field-ops customers wanted to know whether their technicians were where they said they were. The old build leaned into surveillance: every minute, every row, every manager. It worked technically and failed culturally — adoption was sinking.

§ 01 — Context

A product caught between two truths.

Operations managers were under board-level pressure to reduce "ghost visits" — tasks technicians marked complete from the wrong location, sometimes from home. The first version of the product surfaced this as a wall-of-data: 800-row tables, refresh every minute, a column for every breach.

Two things were happening at once:

  • Managers weren't catching malpractice — the signal was buried in the noise.
  • Technicians felt watched, not trusted — uninstall rates were climbing.

Both sides were paying customers of the same screen. The screen had to change for both.

§ 02 — Problem

The shape of the problem was dignity, not data.

I framed it for the room as a one-liner: we are designing a lie-detector and selling it as a productivity tool. The team flinched, then agreed. That framing reset the brief: stop optimising the detection UI; start optimising the experience of being inside the system.

I'm not lying. I parked across the street because the customer's lane was closed. The app pinged my supervisor before I got out of the truck. — Tech, F2F session, Pune
§ 03 — Research

Both sides, same week.

I refused to run only the manager interviews — that's how the first build had gone wrong. Over two weeks I ran:

A 8 ride-alongs with field technicians Observed real visits across three cities. Logged every false-positive alert, every "had to call my supervisor" moment, every reason a row went red that wasn't actually malpractice. Field
B 6 manager shadowing sessions Sat next to ops managers during their daily review. Counted clicks. Most managers were ignoring the breach column entirely — overwhelmed. Web
C 2 anonymous tech surveys Captured the half nobody says out loud: 64% felt the app's tone was punitive. 41% had a workaround for at least one alert. Quant

The cross-cut: most "breaches" were honest. Wrong parking lot. Customer cancelled, next visit early. Geofence drawn lazily by an ops admin who'd never been there. The system was treating noise as crime.

§ 04 — Principles

The four lines I wrote before any pixel.

01 Nudge the tech first, not the manager. Any check the system can do, do it on the technician's phone. The manager only sees what the tech could not resolve themselves. Two-sided
02 Calm signals beat red rows. Replace the wall-of-table with a live map. Spatial sense is faster than scanning. Severity by colour, not row count. Manager
03 Always provide an out. Every alert has a one-tap "I'm at the right place — here's why." Captures context. Removes the need to call. Tech
04 Truth, not surveillance. Show technicians the same map their manager sees. No private dashboards. Symmetry kills the paranoia. Trust
§ 05 — Technician surface

A calm in-pocket guide, not a tracker.

The mobile redesign hinges on one moment: when the tech arrives near a site. Before, the app said nothing; after a silent grace period, the manager got a red row. Now, the tech gets a gentle haptic and a single card:

Before · silent → manager alert
mobile · old tasks list
After · gentle in-app nudge
mobile · geofence nudge

The card is short, never red, and gives three responses: I'll move, I'm actually here — here's why, or customer site moved. Every reply trains the geofence so the same false positive doesn't happen tomorrow.

§ 06 — Manager surface

From 800-row table to one calm map.

The manager dashboard was the most-opened screen at the customer and the least-loved. I cut it down to one view:

The manager opens the dashboard and reads one number in two seconds: how many of my people need me right now? Travel routes draw themselves; geofences are visible as soft rings; only the unresolved outsiders are warm-coloured.

§ 07 — Geo-fence system

A fence the tech can argue with.

Most enterprise location tools draw a static circle and call it a day. We needed something that learned from honest dissent — the tech who keeps getting flagged because the geofence was drawn 30m off the real entrance.

geofence · resolution policy
{
  "site_id": "acme-mum-warehouse-2",
  "shape": "poly",
  "radius_m": 35,
  "grace_min": 3,           // silence before any nudge
  "on_breach": [
    { "step": "haptic",    "who": "tech" },
    { "step": "nudge_card", "who": "tech", "options": [
        "i_will_move", "im_actually_here", "site_moved"
      ]
    },
    { "step": "manager_pin", "who": "manager",
      "only_if": "no_tech_reply > 5min" }
  ],
  "learns_from": ["im_actually_here", "site_moved"]
}

A tech who taps "I'm actually here" three times in a week causes the polygon to widen automatically; the next admin sees the suggestion in the geofence editor. The fence stops being a static argument the technician keeps losing.

§ 09 — Learnings

What I'd carry into the next role.

Two-sided products need two-sided research, in the same week. Otherwise you tune the loudest customer and break the silent one.

Calm beats correct. A noisy true-positive that triggers a defensive phone call is worse than a quiet one the system resolves itself.

Let the people inside the system fix the system. The most valuable feature was a button that disagreed with us. It paid for itself in two weeks.
