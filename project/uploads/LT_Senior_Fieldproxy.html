trending_down THE HIDDEN PROBLEM

Why Fieldproxy Was Losing Enterprise Deals

In Q2 2023, Fieldproxy had a product problem that wasn't obvious from the outside. Our AI dispatch engine was best-in-class. Our mobile app was loved by technicians. But when we tried to win enterprise accounts (100+ technician operations), we kept losing deals to Skedulo and Verizon Connect.

The reason? Enterprise managers couldn't see what their technicians were actually doing in the field in real-time. Without live location tracking, Fieldproxy felt like a scheduling tool, not an operations command center. Large companies needed to trust their field operations. We couldn't provide that visibility.

Our CROs estimated we were losing ~$4M in annual enterprise pipeline because location tracking was a table-stakes requirement in RFPs. Competitors had it. We didn't. It was a blocker in every large deal conversation.

What Enterprise Customers Actually Needed

  • →

    Real-time visibility: "Where are my technicians right now?" Not historical. Not delayed. Live.

  • →

    Trust verification: "Did John actually go to that job site?" Location + timestamp as proof.

  • →

    Operational decisions: "Can we reassign this job to someone closer?" Need live location to make this decision.

  • →

    Compliance & payroll: "Track hours and locations for labor audits and billing accuracy."

The Stakes: Location tracking was preventing us from selling upmarket. It wasn't a nice-to-have feature—it was a prerequisite to the conversation. Without it, we couldn't compete for $500K+ ARR accounts.

How I Approached the Problem

The Core Design Question

"How do we give enterprise managers real-time field visibility and trust while respecting technician privacy and working within the technical constraints of real-world GPS?"

Every design decision was tested against this question. Not just "show the map"—but "show the map in a way that creates trust without surveillance."

1

Validate the Real Problem

I didn't just assume "location tracking." I interviewed 15 field managers, dispatchers, and technicians across 3 customer sites to understand what decisions actually require real-time visibility.

2

Map Competing Needs

Managers want oversight. Technicians fear surveillance. Engineering is concerned about battery drain and data costs. I had to design a system that satisfied all three stakeholders.

3

Design With Constraints

GPS is ±50m accurate in cities. Battery matters. Data storage is expensive. Rather than pretend these don't exist, I designed the entire experience around them.

search RESEARCH & VALIDATION

What I Learned From Talking to Users

Customer Interview Program (15 users across 3 sites)

I conducted structured interviews with field managers (7), dispatchers (4), and technicians (4) from Fieldproxy's customer base. Here's what I learned:

Manager Pain Point #1: "When a customer calls and says the technician isn't here, I spend 15 minutes finding out if they actually sent someone or if there was a dispatch error." The real problem: location + task verification took hours.

Manager Pain Point #2: "I can't reassign jobs in real-time because I don't know where my team is." They were making dispatch decisions blind.

Technician Concern: "I don't want to be watched 24/7." The fear wasn't about location itself—it was about persistent surveillance. They wanted contextual visibility (tracked only during active tasks, not breaks).

Technical Reality: In urban environments, GPS accuracy drops to ±50-80 meters. In rural areas it's better, but still not exact. Showing coordinates as if they were precise was misleading.

Competitive Analysis (How Others Did It Wrong)

I analyzed how Skedulo, Verizon Connect, and ServiceMax handled location:

What They Got Right: They all showed live maps with technician dots. Users could see where the team was.

What They Got Wrong: They showed a dumb map. No context. Just dots. Managers still had to manually check: "Is this person on-task? Have they completed the job? Are they supposed to be here?" The map didn't answer real questions.

Our Opportunity: Competitors showed location. We could show location + context. Location answered "WHERE." We could answer "WHAT, WHY, and WHEN."

Usability Testing: 3 Concepts, 8 Users

Before building anything, I tested three different approaches:

Concept A: Full-Screen Map (Like Google Maps)

Big map on left, activity list on right. Click technician, see details.

Result: ❌ 2/8 preferred. Users lost task context. The map was too important, the task list too secondary. Didn't answer "what are they doing?"

Concept B: List-First, Map as Secondary

Activity list primary view. Click a technician, map shows their location as detail panel.

Result: ⚠️ 4/8 preferred. Better for task management, but users missed spatial awareness. "I can't see clusters of my team in the same area" was feedback from larger operations.

Concept C: Synchronized Dual View (Map + Activity Card)

Map on left showing live locations. Right side shows synchronized activity card. Selecting a technician updates both views simultaneously.

Result: ✅ 6/8 preferred. Users got both spatial AND contextual awareness. The map answered "WHERE." The card answered "WHAT & WHY."

Bonus: Task completion time was 35% faster with Concept C. Users could make decisions more quickly because all the context was visible.

engineering REAL-WORLD CONSTRAINTS

How I Designed WITH Limitations, Not AGAINST Them

Technical Constraints

⚡ Battery vs. Real-Time

GPS updates every 30 seconds minimum. Faster = device dies in 4-6 hours. Managers want "real-time" but battery life matters.

My Solution:

Context-aware refresh: 10-second updates during active task (high attention), 60-second during break (less critical), 5-minute when offline (queue locally). Users felt real-time without battery death.

📍 GPS Accuracy (±50-80m in cities)

You can't show exact coordinates. A technician at coordinates 40.712°N, 74.008°W might actually be 50m away.

My Solution:

Location zones: "At Job Site" > "In Transit" > "Outside Assigned Area". This is honest about GPS accuracy while still providing decision-making data.

💾 Data Storage Costs

Each location point costs ~$0.0005 to store. For 1,000 technicians every 30 seconds, that's $500+/month for 30 days.

My Solution:

Tiered retention: 7 days full resolution (real-time needs), then compress to 30-day summary (compliance needs). This cut storage by 60% while keeping what customers needed.

Privacy & Trust Constraints

👁️ Surveillance vs. Transparency

Always-on location tracking creates resentment. Technicians feel like they're being watched. This drives churn.

My Solution:

Activity-scoped visibility: Location tracked ONLY during assigned tasks. Breaks, lunch, commute time = hidden. Managers get oversight. Technicians feel respected.

⚖️ GDPR/CCPA Compliance

Location data is sensitive. Need explicit consent, right to deletion, transparency.

My Solution:

Consent & control layer: Technicians toggle location per shift. Managers see only active hours. Compliance team can generate audit logs. Trust built into the system.

🔐 Data Security

Location + identity + work patterns = powerful dataset. If compromised, huge liability for customers.

My Solution:

Role-based visibility: Dispatchers see only their team. Managers see their branch. Admins see all. Reduces blast radius if credentials compromised.

balance STRATEGIC TRADE-OFFS

3 Key Decisions That Shaped the Product

Decision 1: Show Real Names vs. Anonymized

The Dilemma: Show "John Smith" or "Technician #42"? Real names enable quick action. Anonymized protects privacy.

Real Names Wins:

  • • Managers can call John directly
  • • Payroll/HR requires clear identity
  • • Compliance audits need names

Risks:

  • • Feels like surveillance to technicians
  • • Data breach = identity leak

What I Tested: 8 managers saw both versions. 7/8 chose real names (operational need > privacy concern). But technicians were uncomfortable.

✅ Decision: Show real names WITH consent model. Each technician agrees to tracking. Transparency about data usage built trust better than anonymity.

Decision 2: Auto-Alerts vs. Manual Thresholds

The Dilemma: Auto-alert managers when technician inactive 10+ min? Prevents issues but creates alert fatigue.

The Math: If we auto-alert for inactivity, a typical manager gets 60+ alerts/day (breaks, lunch, customer delays, traffic). Users ignore alerts when overwhelmed.

✅ Decision: NO automatic alerts. Instead: Configurable thresholds. "Alert me if someone is inactive 30+ min OUTSIDE their assigned job site." Prevents alert fatigue while staying useful.

Decision 3: Historical Playback vs. Task Timeline

The Dilemma: Should managers replay a technician's entire day (minute-by-minute path) or just see task-level summaries?

Reality Check: Playback is cool but managers rarely use it. They want answers to specific questions: "Did John go to job site X?" or "How long was he on-site?" A task timeline answers 95% of real questions with 20% of the complexity.

✅ Decision: Built task timeline (not playback). Shows what happened, when, and where. 80% of value, 20% of complexity.

design_services DESIGN EXECUTION

From Strategy to Interface

Core Components Built

📍 Real-Time Location Map

Live technician locations with status overlays. Color-coded by task state: Active (green), Idle (blue), Offline (gray). Designed for quick scanning.

🎯 Activity Card Component

Synchronized with map selection. Shows technician name, current task, time on-site, last activity. Answers: "What is John doing right now?"

📅 Field Activity Timeline

Chronological view: job assigned → arrived → completed → left. Shows timestamps, location, duration. Answers: "What actually happened?"

🔍 Task Details Panel

Deep-dive into a specific job: location, work done, time spent, notes. Designed for dispute resolution and compliance audits.

Activity Card prioritizes decision-relevant information with clear visual hierarchy: identity → current task → time/location signals → drill-down.

Information Architecture Principle

Every interface followed this hierarchy:

  1. 1. Decision Point: "What's happening with this technician?"
  2. 2. Supporting Data: Time, location, task details
  3. 3. Drill-Down: For disputes or audits, go deeper

This hierarchy forced prioritization. Not everything fits on the first view. What's actually needed to make a decision? That goes first.

lightbulb WHAT I LEARNED

Key Insights & What I'd Do Differently

💡 Insight 1: Constraints Are Features

I initially saw GPS accuracy limits as a problem. I ended up seeing them as the reason our solution was better than competitors' dumb maps. Constraints forced clarity.

💡 Insight 2: Transparency Builds Trust (Better Than Anonymity)

I worried technicians would reject real-time tracking. But honest communication about what data we collect, why, and how to control it mattered more than hiding the feature.

💡 Insight 3: Design Leadership = Defining the Problem

The impact came from identifying the problem (enterprise churn due to location visibility gap) and building the business case, not just designing a pretty interface. Problem definition was the leverage point.

💡 Insight 4: Context Matters More Than Data

Competitors showed location. We succeeded because we showed location + task + time + context. The map alone wasn't the win. The decision-support system was.

What I'd Do Differently Next Time

  • 1.

    Involve technicians earlier in the process. I focused heavily on manager and compliance needs. Technician feedback came mid-way and changed the design significantly. Starting there would have saved iteration.

  • 2.

    Measure privacy perception from day one. We tracked "% tracking enabled" but didn't measure technician trust or perception of surveillance. Building this metric would have shown us earlier that activity-scoped visibility was the breakthrough.

  • 3.

    Plan for 50K scale from day one, not 10K. By month 3, we had growth past initial planning. Building for exponential growth upfront would have prevented scaling pains later.
